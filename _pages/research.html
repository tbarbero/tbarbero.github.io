Tropical Cyclones and Models

I spent a summer as a CIMES intern at Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory and Princeton. I was hosted by Jan-Huey Chen on a project looking at Tropical Cyclone (TC) tracks in global high-resolution models, specifically GFDL's SHiELD, NCEP's GFS, and ECMWF's IFS models. To understand why models simulated movement differently, we look at the effect of the global steering flow on several TC events in 2017 using a method called potential vorticity (PV) inversion. Essentially, we calculate the distribution of potential vorticity, q, and then solve for the distribution of geopotential height and streamfunction...

Dust Storms and the Salton Sea

During my undergraduate studies, I worked under Professor Amato Evan. We were interested in studying dust in this region. This basin is particularily interesting because residents here are often effected by dust storms forced by downslope windstorms as a result of the prevailing westerly flow over the Laguna Mountains. Interestingly, one of California's largest lakes, the Salton Sea, lies at the center and lowest point of the basin. It is characteristically shallow (a max depth of about 15m), saline (50% saltier than seawater) and is rapidly shrinking. This shrinking leads to a disproportionate amount of surface area of dry lakebed (playa) exposed to the atmosphere, possibly contributing fuel for more intense dust storms. As the region becomes more arid and more playa is exposed to the atmosphere, some questions of interest arise: How will dust storms change in terms of frequency and intensity? What are the core dynamics behind these storms and what atmospheric conditions allow for them to occur? Will these changes mean worse effects for residents living in the region?
As a lab assistant for a little over a year, my responsibilities included fieldwork, instrumentation and managing data flow. Specifically, I was in charge of utilizing a GPS-receiver to retrieve precipitable water vapor (PWV). Satellite signals are refracted and thereby delayed by both dry and moist components in the atmosphere. The idea is that we can solve for the amount of moisture (PWV) in the atmosphere by isolating the signal delay solely caused by moisture. Many factors went into this project including, understanding the mathematical theory in converting signal delay to moisture, engineering an antenna mount and configuring the GPS hardware, creating a data flow between the GPS and our server, along with several early-morning fieldwork trips to the Salton Basin. Finally, to process our GPS data into PWV I collaborated with UCARs SuomiNet program resulting in PWV measurements in the Salton Basin. I even had the opportunity to launch weather balloons during intense dust storm events! (Well not pictured below...)

One instrument we have mounted at our site is a sun photometer. We noticed that on days with low wind speeds, i,e., days without dust storms forced by high winds speeds, there was still a moderate signal of dust indicated by aerosol optical depth measurements from the sun photometer. From here, we call this dust, background dust. I worked on understanding the dynamics behind this cycle of background dust in the Salton Basin. I used an array of observations including surface meteorological and air quality measurements, columnar lidar and aerosol optical depth, satellite imagery and upper air soundings. Through a comprehensive analysis, I found that dust suspended near the surface was advected throuhout the basin by mountain-valley flow and is vertically mixed upward as a result of dry convection with the deepening of the boundary layer, both following the diurnal solar cycle. I presented this work at the 2020 AGU conference.
